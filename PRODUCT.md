# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Students in Peru preparing for university admission exams ("preuniversitarios"), typically in their last year of secondary school or enrolled in an intensive prep academy ("academia preuniversitaria"). They are targeting one or more specific universities (UNI, UNMSM/San Marcos, PUCP, UNALM, UNFV, etc.), each with its own exam format, date, and topic weighting. Their context is focused, repeated study sessions — often daily, often under the pressure of a visible exam deadline — mixing short topic drills with full timed mock exams ("simulacros"). They are used to the demanding, no-nonsense tone of an academia classroom, not a consumer app.

## Product Purpose

MatePre helps preuniversitario students practice math for their admission exam(s) and see their progress clearly enough to trust it. It personalizes content by the university (or universities) a student is targeting, keeps their exam date(s) visible via countdowns, and structures practice through weekly goals, per-topic drilling, and timed simulacros that mirror the real exam experience (including per-question timing). It adds a light social layer — an anonymous, opt-in leaderboard — for motivation without compromising privacy. Success looks like: a student practices consistently in the weeks before their exam, knows exactly which topics they're weak in, and walks into the real exam having already rehearsed its exact conditions.

## Brand Personality

Serious and trustworthy without being cold — like a good academia teacher: demanding but approachable. Three words: **riguroso, cercano, claro** (rigorous, approachable, clear). Clarity beats decoration on every screen; the interface should feel like well-kept class notes, not a consumer app trying to entertain. The product already has habit-building mechanics (streaks, weekly goals, a leaderboard) — these should read as quiet accountability tools, not gamified/toy-like flourishes. Avoid tipping into a playful, badge-covered, confetti-heavy register; the tone stays academic even where the mechanics are game-like.

## Anti-references

No specific named anti-references given. The one explicit boundary: don't drift into an energetic/gamified visual language (bright celebratory motion, cartoon badges, playful mascots) even though streaks/goals/ranking exist — keep those features dressed in the same rigorous, notebook-like visual system as the rest of the app.

## Design Principles

- **Clarity over decoration.** Every screen should read like clean class notes: information hierarchy and legibility win over visual flourish.
- **Calm under time pressure.** Timers, countdowns, and exam-session moments should feel precise and steady, never chaotic or alarming — the product's job is to lower exam anxiety through rehearsal, not add to it.
- **Progress motivates, it never shames.** Streaks, weekly goals, and comparisons to the average must always read as encouraging accountability, not guilt or public failure.
- **One university, one context.** A student can target multiple universities with different exams and dates; every screen showing content, filters, or recommendations must make it unambiguous which university's context is being shown.
- **Anonymity is a feature, not an afterthought.** Any social or competitive surface (ranking, comparisons) is opt-in and pseudonymous by design — never leaks real names or identifying data.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Practical WCAG AA-level defaults: verified color contrast, comfortable touch targets, and reduced-motion alternatives for any animation. No formal compliance mandate or additional specific accommodations were requested beyond this reasonable baseline.
